# Industry: Academia / Research

Applies to: PhD program applications, postdoc applications, tenure-track faculty, research scientist at government / national labs (NASA, Fermilab, CERN, Max Planck, CNRS, NIH-intramural), industry research labs (FAIR, DeepMind, MSR, NVIDIA Research) when they explicitly expect a CV.

Academic documents are **CVs, not résumés**. The rules of concision do not apply — completeness and verifiability do.

## CV vs. résumé decision
- **PhD / postdoc / faculty / grant applications**: CV (unlimited length; 3–15 pages typical).
- **Research scientist at an industry lab**: CV or résumé — the job posting will usually specify. If it says "CV", produce a CV; if "résumé", produce an industry-style 2-pager per the tech industry reference.

## Length
- Early-career (new PhD): 3–5 pages
- Mid-career postdoc: 5–8 pages
- Faculty / senior research: 10–30+ pages — no cap, but structure matters more

## Section order (CV)
1. Header (full name, affiliation, email, ORCID, personal site, Google Scholar link)
2. Education (with advisor name and thesis title)
3. Academic / Research Positions
4. Publications (the heart of the CV; see below)
5. Preprints / Under Review (separate subsection)
6. Grants & Funding (awarded + pending, amount + role PI/co-PI)
7. Awards, Fellowships, Honors
8. Invited Talks & Conference Presentations
9. Teaching Experience (courses taught, role, institution, term)
10. Mentorship / Supervision (PhD/Masters/undergrad students advised)
11. Professional Service (journal reviewing, area chairing, organizing committees)
12. Workshops / Summer schools attended
13. Memberships (ACM, IEEE, AMS, APS, ACS, etc.)
14. Languages
15. References (3 names, or "available upon request" — but for faculty apps, always list 3)

Some fields prefer: **Publications → Grants → Talks** up top, with Teaching later (hard sciences). Other fields prefer: **Teaching → Publications** for primarily-teaching institutions (SLACs). Follow the discipline's convention.

## Publications formatting — this is where CVs live or die

- Reverse chronological within each category.
- **Categorize**: Refereed Journal Articles / Refereed Conference Papers / Book Chapters / Edited Volumes / Preprints / Other.
- **Author order conventions differ by field**:
  - CS / ML: alphabetical sometimes, but first/last author are most significant. **Bold your name.**
  - Life sciences: first-author and last-author (PI) positions are the ones that matter. Bold your name.
  - Physics / large collaborations: alphabetical author lists are normal — note "[alphabetical]" next to the citation.
  - Economics: strict alphabetical; note position only if it's meaningful in the field.
- **Always include**: full author list (or truncate with "... et al." only for huge-n papers), full title, venue, year, DOI or URL.
- Mark equal contribution with * and a footnote: "* equal contribution".
- Under-review / in-preparation: list separately; do **not** inflate by calling them publications.

Example (CS/ML):
> **Mercer, J. K.**, Huang, L., & Rossi, E. (2024). "Adaptive Retrieval-Augmented Language Models for Long-Context Reasoning." *Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems (NeurIPS 2024)*.

Example (life sciences):
> Smith, J., **Doe, A.**, Chen, L., & Patel, R. (2024). "Role of mRNA cap-analog modifications on LNP delivery efficiency." *Nature Biotechnology*, 42(8), 1123–1135. https://doi.org/10.1038/nbt.xxxx

## Grants section

Include role, title, agency, grant number, amount, dates, and (if available) success rate.

> *Co-PI, "Retrieval-Augmented Language Models for Scientific Search", NSF III-2345678, $480,000, 2024–2027.*
> *PI, Seed grant, University Center for Applied AI, $12,000, 2023.*

Pending grants go under "Pending" subsection. Declined grants are not listed.

## Awards & Fellowships

- Prize / fellowship name, awarding body, year, amount (if notable), and selectivity (e.g., "1 of 120 awardees from 950 applicants" — if verifiable).
- Travel grants matter early-career; stop listing them once senior.

## Invited talks

> *Mercer, J. K. "Retrieval-augmented language models in production." Invited talk, Stanford NLP Seminar, June 2025.*

Distinguish **invited** from **contributed/accepted** talks.

## Teaching

| Role    | Course                             | Institution        | Term        |
|---------|------------------------------------|--------------------|-------------|
| TA      | Machine Perception (MSc)           | Northern Cross U.  | Fall 2024   |
| Instr.  | Seminar: Neural Retrieval (MSc)    | Northern Cross U.  | Spring 2024 |

Include size of cohort and your teaching evaluation score if it's strong (rare in DE/AT, common in the US).

## Mentorship

List supervised students by type (PhD / Master / Bachelor / Project), their thesis title, year, and (if relevant) what they're doing now.

## Professional service

- Reviewer for: [journals/conferences, with years]
- Area chair / program committee: [venue, year]
- Workshop organizer: [title, venue, year]
- Best-reviewer awards — worth listing if from top venue

## Field-specific adjustments

- **Math / theoretical CS**: often include "Research interests" paragraph after education; list "Manuscripts in preparation" less liberally.
- **Humanities**: include "Selected research projects" section; publications take longer, so include conference talks more prominently.
- **Biomedical**: always include an ORCID; include clinical-rotation or wet-lab-skill inventory if applying for a position expecting them.
- **Engineering / industry-lab research scientist roles**: add a "Patents" section and a "Technical skills" block with software/tools — closer to tech résumé style.

## References section

Three references for junior applications; five for faculty search. Include: name, title, institution, email, relationship (e.g., "PhD advisor", "Thesis committee member").

Confirm with each referee **before** listing. Do not invent or list without permission.

## What NOT to do
- Do not cut content to force one or two pages — a CV is supposed to be complete.
- Do not inflate preprints to count as peer-reviewed publications.
- Do not hide a failed / withdrawn paper by restyling it as a "tech report" — if it's tech-report-quality, cite the tech-report number.
- Do not list "Reviewed 100+ papers" without breaking down venues.
- Do not put hobbies on an academic CV unless directly relevant (e.g., musician applying for a music-theory role).
